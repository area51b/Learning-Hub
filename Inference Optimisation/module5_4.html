Dynamo vs LLM-d vs DIY - The Decision Framework

When to recommend each approach, how to handle the "we built our own" customer, the real cost of DIY orchestration, and what the convergence between Dynamo and llm-d means for your recommendations.

Modules 5.2 and 5.3 covered what Dynamo and llm-d are. This module is about how to choose between them - and what to say when the answer is neither because the customer has already built something themselves. In practice, the most important conversation is not "Dynamo or llm-d?" but "should this customer adopt any fleet coordination framework at all, and if so, which one fits their existing infrastructure and team?"

There is also a third option that appears constantly in customer conversations: the team has already built a bespoke routing and disaggregation layer in-house. Understanding the real cost of that approach - and how to frame a migration conversation - is just as important as knowing the technical differences between the two frameworks. And one more development that shapes all of these conversations: NVIDIA is actively contributing Dynamo components (NIXL, the Planner, the KV Cache Manager) into the llm-d project, which means the two are more complementary than competitive.

1 The Convergence Reality - These Projects Are Collaborating, Not Competing
▾

The framing of "Dynamo vs llm-d" is accurate for understanding architectural differences but misleading for understanding the ecosystem trajectory. In August 2025, NVIDIA announced active collaboration with the llm-d community: NIXL is already shared between both, and NVIDIA committed to integrating the Dynamo Planner and KV Cache Manager directly into llm-d. This means llm-d is progressively gaining Dynamo's most advanced capabilities while maintaining its Kubernetes-native operational model.

What this means for customer conversations: When a customer asks "should we use Dynamo or llm-d?", the honest answer increasingly is "if you are on NVIDIA hardware and want Kubernetes-native operations, you may be able to get the best of both - llm-d's operational model with Dynamo's performance components integrated." The decision framework still matters, but frame it as "which coordination model fits your infrastructure" rather than "which project is better."

3 The DIY Problem - What "We Built Our Own" Really Costs
▾

Many teams at large-scale deployments arrive at the fleet coordination problem and solve it in-house - a custom router, some scripts for KV transfer, a homegrown Prefill/Decode coordinator. This is understandable: both Dynamo and llm-d were announced in 2025, and teams with 2023-2024 scale problems had no standard framework to adopt. But the cost of maintaining bespoke orchestration compounds over time in ways that are not obvious at the start.

This estimate reflects the experience of teams that have built bespoke fleet orchestration at hyperscaler scale. The initial build cost is visible and planned for. The ongoing maintenance, topology adaptation, and ecosystem feature gap costs are the ones that make DIY uncompetitive over a 2-3 year horizon. By contrast, adopting Dynamo or llm-d means these improvements are contributed upstream - KV offload tiers, predicted latency scheduling, LoRA routing were added by the project communities at zero marginal cost to adopters.
The migration conversation framing: When a customer says "we already built our own," the productive question is not "was that a mistake?" but "how does your solution compare to what Dynamo/llm-d now provides out of the box, and what is your plan to keep pace as these frameworks add capabilities?" Most teams find that their bespoke solution handles 60-70% of what the frameworks now provide - but the remaining 30-40% (predicted latency scheduling, KV offload tiers, LoRA routing, active-active HA) would take significant engineering to build, and the frameworks already have it. The ROI of migration becomes clear when mapped against the roadmap delta.
4 Migration Paths - From Current Stack to Fleet Coordination
▾

Neither Dynamo nor llm-d requires a big-bang migration. Both are designed for incremental adoption. The right migration sequence depends on starting point and urgency - here are the three most common paths:

1
From: vLLM + round-robin LB → To: llm-d Well-lit Path 1 (intelligent scheduling)
Deploy the Kubernetes Inference Gateway and EPP in front of existing vLLM pods. No changes to vLLM configuration, no new GPU provisioning, no disaggregation. The EPP becomes the new routing layer - existing vLLM pods register with the InferencePool CRD. Existing Kubernetes tooling (Helm, Prometheus, Grafana) continues to work. Lowest risk migration available.
Expected gain: 2-3x TTFT improvement from prefix cache hit restoration. Timeline: 1-2 weeks for a Kubernetes-experienced team.
2
From: vLLM + Triton → To: Dynamo (router + planner, keep vLLM backend)
Deploy Dynamo's frontend and KV-aware router in front of existing vLLM pods. Triton's serving role is replaced by Dynamo's frontend - existing model configuration moves to Dynamo's backend definitions. vLLM configuration is unchanged. Once stable, add the Planner and enable disaggregation by splitting pods into Prefill and Decode roles. Requires InfiniBand validation before enabling NIXL transfer.
Expected gain: 3-4x TTFT (KV router) + 1.5-2x throughput (disaggregation). Timeline: 2-4 weeks for Dynamo router; add 4-6 weeks for disaggregation with testing.
3
From: TRT-LLM + Triton → To: TRT-LLM + Dynamo (full stack)
TRT-LLM stays as the inference engine - Dynamo is positioned as the fleet coordination layer above it, replacing Triton's routing role. TRT-LLM compiled engines are unchanged. Dynamo's router handles multi-replica routing with KV awareness. Planner manages dynamic P:D allocation across engine replicas. This is the highest-performance path but has the most operational change - new frontend process, new routing configuration, new monitoring to set up.
Expected gain: highest possible throughput on NVIDIA hardware. Timeline: 4-8 weeks. Requires dedicated ML platform engineer with Dynamo familiarity.
4
From: Bespoke DIY orchestration → To: Framework (either)
The highest-friction migration because the team has existing custom code that the organisation depends on. Best approach: run the framework in shadow mode alongside the bespoke system for 2-4 weeks, comparing routing decisions and validating latency parity. Once the framework matches or exceeds bespoke performance, cut over one replica group at a time. Document the capability delta (what the framework provides that the bespoke system does not) to justify the migration to stakeholders.
Expected gain: elimination of ongoing maintenance burden + access to framework roadmap features. Timeline: 6-12 weeks including shadow validation and staged cutover.

6 The Three-Question Diagnostic - Identifying the Right Answer Quickly
▾

In a customer conversation, you rarely have time to work through a full framework comparison. These three questions surface the most important decision factors in under two minutes:

Question 1 - What is your GPU fleet?
All NVIDIA? Both Dynamo and llm-d are viable. Go to Question 2.
Mixed or non-NVIDIA? llm-d is the only viable fleet coordination framework. Dynamo is not designed for AMD/Intel/TPU.
Fewer than 16 GPUs? Neither framework is justified yet. The Part 4 stack is right for this scale.
Question 2 - What does your existing routing and gateway layer look like?
Yes, with existing Envoy/Gateway API routing layer: llm-d slots directly into it - the EPP is just a filter added to Envoy. Zero new routing infrastructure needed.
Yes K8s, but no existing Envoy/Gateway API layer: Both Dynamo and llm-d deploy via Helm with CRDs and operators. Go to hardware and workload criteria to decide.
TRT-LLM in stack or NVIDIA AI Enterprise required: Dynamo is stronger - native TRT-LLM support, Blackwell optimisation, enterprise support contract.
Question 3 - What is the primary symptom driving the conversation?
TTFT variance / prefix cache not working at scale: Either framework - KV-aware routing is the fix. Go with whichever fits Q1 and Q2.
Maximum throughput on reasoning models: Dynamo on Blackwell is the strongest answer. The 30x claim is real for this specific case.
Disaggregation coordination too complex / manual: Either framework solves this, llm-d via sidecar + scheduler, Dynamo via Planner.
Operational complexity reduction / standardisation: llm-d's well-lit paths and Kubernetes-native ops model is specifically designed for this.
The answer that is almost never wrong: When in doubt, recommend llm-d Well-lit Path 1 as the first step for any Kubernetes-native team with multiple vLLM replicas and prefix cache problems. It is the lowest-risk adoption, it directly addresses the most common symptom (TTFT variance from routing blindness), and it does not lock the team out of adopting Dynamo components later - because NVIDIA is actively contributing Dynamo capabilities into llm-d anyway.
7 4 Key Ideas for Customer Conversations
▾
01
The first question is scale - neither framework adds value below ~16 GPUs with multiple replicas. Don't recommend fleet coordination to teams that haven't hit fleet-scale problems
Dynamo and llm-d both add operational complexity. That complexity is justified by the performance gains at fleet scale. Below the threshold where Module 5.1's problems manifest, the Part 4 stack is right, and adding a coordination layer is premature optimisation. The diagnostic conversation should establish scale before any framework discussion.
02
GPU fleet composition is the tie-breaker - any non-NVIDIA hardware in the fleet makes llm-d the only viable choice
Dynamo's performance advantages are real on NVIDIA, especially Blackwell. But they are NVIDIA-specific. A team with AMD MI300X, Google TPU, or Intel GPU in the fleet cannot use Dynamo to coordinate the full fleet. llm-d is the only fleet coordination framework designed for hardware-agnostic operation. This is a hard constraint, not a preference.
03
DIY orchestration has a compounding cost that is not visible at build time - the migration conversation should focus on the roadmap delta, not the initial build quality
Bespoke fleet orchestration teams have usually built something functional. The productive question is not whether it was worth building, but whether the team can afford to keep it at parity with what Dynamo and llm-d are adding to their roadmaps each quarter - predicted latency scheduling, KV offload tiers, LoRA routing, active-active HA. Most teams cannot, and the maintenance burden grows each time a new optimisation is needed.
04
Dynamo and llm-d are converging - NVIDIA is contributing Dynamo components into llm-d, so the choice is increasingly about operational model, not technology access
NIXL is already in llm-d. The Dynamo Planner and KV Cache Manager are planned for llm-d integration. This means teams choosing llm-d for its Kubernetes-native operations model are not sacrificing Dynamo's performance technology - they are getting it through a different operational interface. The enduring choice is between centralised Planner coordination (Dynamo) and Kubernetes-native sidecar coordination (llm-d), not between accessing good or bad technology.
MODULE 5.4 - DECISION FRAMEWORK CHEAT SHEET
Scale threshold
Fleet coordination frameworks add value at ~16+ GPUs with multiple replicas. Below this, the Part 4 stack is sufficient. Never recommend Dynamo or llm-d to a single-node team.
GPU fleet hard rule
Any AMD, Intel, or Google TPU in the fleet = llm-d only. Dynamo is NVIDIA-optimised. This is a constraint, not a preference. Mixed fleets have no Dynamo option.
Routing layer rule
Both Dynamo and llm-d are K8s-native - Helm, CRDs, operators, Prometheus. The real differentiator: existing Envoy/Gateway API layer = llm-d slots in with zero new routing infra. No existing gateway layer = both equally valid; decide on hardware and engine criteria instead.
Convergence reality
NVIDIA contributes NIXL, Planner, and KV Cache Manager into llm-d. The technology gap is closing. The enduring difference is coordination model: centralised Planner (Dynamo) vs K8s-native sidecar (llm-d).
DIY migration trigger
Ask: "Can your team maintain parity with the framework roadmap?" If not, the migration ROI is the delta between ongoing maintenance cost vs framework adoption. Roadmap features already in frameworks: predicted latency scoring, KV offload tiers, LoRA routing, HA.
Safest first recommendation
llm-d Well-lit Path 1 (intelligent scheduling only) for any Kubernetes team with multiple vLLM replicas + TTFT variance. Lowest risk, directly fixes routing blindness, does not block future Dynamo component adoption.
When 30x is real
DeepSeek-R1 671B, NVIDIA GB200 NVL72, full disaggregation enabled. H100 realistic range: 3-6x with KV router + disaggregation. Still significant - but context matters in customer conversations.
Migration order
1. KV-aware router (immediate win, lowest risk). 2. Add Planner/disaggregation after InfiniBand validated. 3. Full P/D split last - most complex, highest gain. Never skip to step 3 without step 1 showing value.
QUICK CHECK
A customer has 64x NVIDIA H100 SXM GPUs across 8 nodes, running a mix of vLLM and TRT-LLM deployments. They currently use a bespoke Python routing script written 18 months ago that does basic load-balancing and a static 2:6 Prefill:Decode split. The script requires a senior engineer to manually adjust the P:D ratio when traffic patterns change, and has broken twice in the past 6 months when they updated TRT-LLM. They ask: "Should we adopt Dynamo, llm-d, or fix our existing script?" Which response most accurately represents the full picture?
A. Fix the existing script - it already does basic load-balancing and P:D splitting, and the frameworks are too new to trust with production traffic at this scale.
B. Adopt llm-d - it is Kubernetes-native and therefore fits any team's operations.
C. This fleet crosses every threshold where a framework is justified: 64 GPUs, mixed vLLM and TRT-LLM, manual P:D adjustment, and demonstrated breakage on engine updates. The bespoke script is exactly the DIY problem described in this module - functional for 60-70% of the job, but with compounding maintenance cost and a growing gap against framework capabilities. The question is which framework. Since this is an all-NVIDIA fleet with both vLLM and TRT-LLM in use, Dynamo is the stronger fit: it natively supports both engines, its Planner provides the dynamic P:D adjustment that currently requires manual intervention, and NVIDIA AI Enterprise provides the enterprise support contract that justifies replacing production-critical bespoke code. The migration path: deploy Dynamo's KV-aware router in shadow mode alongside the existing script for 2-4 weeks, validate parity, then cut over. Add the Planner for dynamic P:D after the router is stable.
D. Standardise on vLLM only and remove TRT-LLM - using both engines adds complexity and is the root cause of the script breakage.
