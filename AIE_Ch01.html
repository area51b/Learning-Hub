The Rise of AI Engineering

This section introduces the significant shift occurring in the field of artificial intelligence, moving from traditional Machine Learning (ML) Engineering towards a new discipline: AI Engineering. It highlights the key drivers behind this evolution and the foundational changes in how AI applications are conceptualized and built.

  • Shift in Focus: Moving from traditional ML Engineering to AI Engineering.
  • Catalyst: The emergence and widespread availability of powerful Foundation Models (FMs) and Large Language Models (LLMs).
  • Accessibility: FMs and LLMs lower the barrier to entry, enabling more people to build AI applications without deep ML expertise.
  • Product-Centric Approach: AI Engineering often starts with product development and leveraging existing models, rather than data collection and model training.

Foundation Models and Language Models

This section delves into the core components driving the AI engineering revolution: Foundation Models and Language Models. It explains their characteristics, how they function, and their significance in building modern AI applications. Understanding these models is crucial for anyone looking to work in this space.

Foundation Models (FMs):

  • Pre-trained on vast amounts of data.
  • Highly adaptable for various downstream tasks (e.g., coding, writing, chatbots).
  • Dual significance: fundamental importance and adaptability.

Language Models (LMs):

  • Encode statistical information about language(s).
  • Predict the likelihood of a word in a given context.
  • Categories:
    • Masked Language Models (e.g., BERT)
    • Autoregressive Language Models (e.g., GPT in ChatGPT)

Tokens:

The basic unit of a language model (can be a character, a word, or a part of a word). Tokenization is the process of breaking text into these tokens.

The AI Engineering Stack

Understanding the AI Engineering Stack is key to grasping how modern AI applications are structured and developed. This section breaks down the stack into its three primary layers, outlining the focus and responsibilities of each. This layered approach helps organize the complexities involved in building and deploying AI solutions.

1. Application Development Layer

Focus:

Prompt crafting, context provision, interface design, user experience.

Responsibilities:

Evaluation, prompt engineering, AI interface development.

2. Model Development Layer

Focus:

Adapting and fine-tuning models.

Techniques:

Prompt-based (no weight updates) and fine-tuning (requires weight updates).

3. Infrastructure Layer

Focus:

Serving and deploying models efficiently.

Planning AI Applications

Effective planning is paramount for the success of any AI application. This section outlines crucial considerations that teams must address before and during development. It also introduces the "80/20" development pattern, a common observation in AI projects that can help set realistic timelines and resource allocations.

Key Considerations:

  • Use Case Evaluation: Is AI truly needed, or are traditional methods more suitable? (Avoid using AI just because it's available).
  • Setting Expectations: Be realistic about AI capabilities and limitations.
  • User Exposure: Determine how users will interact with the AI.
  • Deployment: Internal vs. external deployment.
  • Maintenance: Plan for ongoing updates and support.

The "80/20" Development Pattern:

Reaching 80% functionality is often quicker than the final 20%, which can require significant effort. This pattern highlights that the last mile of polish and perfection can be disproportionately time-consuming.

Evaluation and Implementation Challenges

Evaluating AI models, especially powerful foundation models, presents a unique set of challenges distinct from traditional software or ML systems. This section explores these difficulties and underscores the critical role of human evaluation in ensuring AI systems are reliable, fair, and perform as intended.

Unique Challenges with AI Models:

  • Open-ended Outputs: Difficulty in defining "correct" answers.
  • Guardrails: Challenges in implementing strict controls.
  • Type Enforcement: Ensuring outputs adhere to expected formats.
  • Comprehensive Strategies: Need for robust evaluation methods beyond traditional ML metrics.

Importance of Human Evaluation:

Essential for sanity checks and validating automatic evaluation methods. Human oversight remains crucial for complex AI systems.

Data and Model Adaptation:

The focus shifts from raw data collection and processing to effective model adaptation and deployment strategies. This means curating and preparing data specifically for fine-tuning or providing context to foundation models.

Modern Development Paradigm & Pitfalls

The advent of AI engineering has ushered in a modern development paradigm that differs significantly from traditional software or ML projects. This section highlights this new product-oriented approach and the evolving skill sets required. It also provides a crucial overview of common pitfalls to avoid when building AI applications.

Modern Approach:

  • Product-Oriented Approach: Start with product requirements, then adapt models.
  • Shift in Skills: Growing recognition that understanding AI system behavior (constraints, opportunities) is valuable for software engineers.

Common Pitfalls (Click to Expand):

Using generative AI when not necessary ▼

Not every problem requires a generative AI solution. Simpler, more traditional methods might be more efficient, reliable, and cost-effective. Evaluate if the complexity of GenAI aligns with the problem's needs.

Confusing "bad product" with "bad AI" ▼

Sometimes, an AI application fails not because the underlying AI model is flawed, but because the product design, user experience, or problem framing is poor. It's crucial to distinguish between AI limitations and product strategy issues.

Starting too complex ▼

Begin with simpler approaches and iterate. Jumping to complex architectures or extensive fine-tuning prematurely can lead to wasted effort and difficulties in debugging and maintenance.

Over-indexing on early success ▼

Impressive demos or initial positive results can be misleading. Transitioning from a proof-of-concept to a robust, production-ready application often uncovers significant challenges (the "80/20" rule applies here too).

Forgoing human evaluation ▼

While automated metrics are useful, human evaluation is indispensable for assessing nuanced aspects like quality, relevance, safety, and fairness, especially for generative models with open-ended outputs.

Crowdsourcing use cases without a clear strategy ▼

While exploring potential applications is good, it should be guided by a strategic vision. Randomly pursuing use cases without aligning them with business goals or user needs can lead to scattered efforts and low-impact outcomes.
